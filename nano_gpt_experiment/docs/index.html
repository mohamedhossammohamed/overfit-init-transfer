Fixed-budget pretraining transfer in small transformers

A pilot study on whether pretraining a small transformer to extreme, fixed-token-budget exposure on one corpus — before training on an unrelated one — changes downstream performance versus training from random initialization.

Design

Condition A
Baseline. Random initialization, trained directly on the target corpus (Tiny Shakespeare subset, English, character-level).
Condition B
Corpus-pretrained. Random initialization, pretrained on a structured Arabic-script corpus with near-zero character overlap with the English target, then finetuned on the target corpus with the same step budget as Condition A.
Condition C
Noise-pretrained. Random initialization, pretrained on uniformly sampled random characters from the shared vocabulary, then finetuned on the target corpus — controls for whether any prior gradient exposure, regardless of structure, produces the same effect.
Scales
Two model sizes: ~106k parameters (2 layers, 4 heads, embed 64) and ~1.98M parameters (4 layers, 8 heads, embed 256). Two random seeds per configuration (42, 1337).
Budget
Fixed at 200 tokens per parameter — approximately 10× the Chinchilla compute-optimal ratio — applied identically to pretraining and finetuning phases. Checkpoints for finetuning are taken at 50% and 90% of the pretraining budget.

Methodology

Training budgets are set in tokens, not by loss threshold: each run receives exactly 200 × param_count tokens. Three conditions (baseline / structured-corpus-pretrained / noise-pretrained) are compared at two model sizes across two random seeds. Final validation loss is compared at matched finetuning step budgets across conditions.

Conditions B and C each produce two finetuning runs from checkpoints at 50% and 90% of the pretraining budget. The baseline (Condition A) uses the full budget as a single training phase. All finetuning runs receive the same total finetuning step count regardless of checkpoint depth.

Tentative reading

At the 2M scale (seed 42, the only complete seed for all three conditions), the corpus-pretrained condition (B) and the noise-pretrained condition (C) converge to statistically similar final validation loss — both underperforming the random-initialization baseline (A). The baseline achieves a final validation loss of 0.3205; the best pretrained conditions reach 0.3648 (C frac 0.9) and 0.3719 (B frac 0.9).

This indicates that, at this scale, prior training exposure on a structured non-English corpus with near-zero character overlap with the target does not confer a measurable downstream benefit over noise pretraining. Both pretrained conditions incur a deficit relative to random initialization under a fixed token budget. The shared-vocabulary structure that the model could in principle have leveraged does not produce a measurable benefit under these conditions.

At the 100k scale (both seeds complete), the pattern differs: noise-pretrained (C) runs slightly outperform the baseline, while corpus-pretrained (B) runs underperform it — though all final losses remain close and all curves were still descending at budget exhaustion.

Caveats. The 2M B-vs-C comparison is single-seed (seed 42 only); seed 1337 replication is in progress. Additionally, per the project's data glossary, seed 42 and seed 1337 use different underlying text for Path B pretraining (the original corpus vs. a byte-and-token-matched replacement Arabic Wikipedia corpus), making them non-exact replicates for Path B specifically. Path A and Path C are unaffected and remain true replicates across seeds. All results should be understood as exploratory pilot data, not confirmatory findings.

Scope

This is a small, character-level pilot — models between 106k and 1.98M parameters, two random seeds per configuration, run on personal hardware. It is exploratory, not confirmatory: a pattern here is a reason to look further, not a finding on its own. Every metric and checkpoint is published as collected, including null or negative results.
